# Chatoyancy (Cat's-Eye Effect)

## Overview

Chatoyancy appears when a gem contains densely packed parallel
fibers, channels, or tubes. On a cabochon cut perpendicular to
the inclusions, a single bright band slides across the dome —
the "cat's eye." The namesake is chrysoberyl (cymophane); the
same effect occurs in tourmaline, apatite, and others. Moonstone
produces a visually similar floating sheen via a different
mechanism (see adularescence).


## Mechanism

Single-direction reflection: all inclusions align along one
direction (e.g., hollow tubes parallel to the c-axis in
chrysoberyl). From above, each tube reflects perpendicular light
to the viewer. The collective reflection is geometrically
constrained to a narrow band perpendicular to the inclusion
direction. Effect strength scales with inclusion density, fineness,
and parallelism.


## Example Gemstones

| Gem | Tubes/Fibers | Host |
|---|---|---|
Chrysoberyl Cat's-eye / 金绿猫眼 | hollow tubes along c-axis | Chrysoberyl
Tourmaline Cat's-eye / 碧玺猫眼 | parallel hollow channels | Elbaite
Apatite Cat's-eye / 磷灰石猫眼 | fibrous inclusions | Apatite
Hawk's-eye / 鹰眼石 | crocidolite fibers | Quartz (pseudomorph)
Tiger's-eye / 虎眼石 | crocidolite fibers (oxidized) | Quartz (pseudomorph)
