# Labradorescence (Spectrolite)

## Overview

The signature effect of labradorite (plagioclase feldspar
series). On a cabochon cut, alternating nanoscale layers or
compositional striations reflect and interfere with light,
producing vivid flashes of blue, green, orange, and gold with
a metallic sheen. The colors shift continuously with viewing
angle — a holographic quality. The mechanism is similar to
adularescence but with greater compositional variation and
refractive-index contrast between layers, producing more
saturated colors. The most famous example is Finnish Spectrolite.


## Mechanism

Compositional lamellar structure: labradorite's anorthite
(CaAl₂Si₂O₈) and albite (NaAlSi₃O₈) sub-layers alternate with
thicknesses of ~50–250 nm. Refractive-index contrasts between
layers (typically 0.005–0.012) fall in the range needed for
strong selective interference with visible light. As viewing
angle changes, the reflected wavelength shifts, producing
angle-dependent color variation.
