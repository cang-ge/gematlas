# Color Cause

Color in gems is never arbitrary. Three primary mechanisms account for nearly all natural colored stones:

## Three Primary Mechanisms

| Mechanism | Summary |
|---|---|
| [Transition-Metal Ion Coloration](transition-metal) | Transition-metal ions (Cr, Fe, Mn, Co, Ni, V, Cu, Ti) have
partially filled d or f orbitals. When substituted into a host
lattice, crystal-field splitting creates d-d absorption bands
in the visible spectrum. The unabsorbed wavelengths produce
the gem's color. This is the dominant cause of color in
gemstones — roughly 70% of colored gems owe their hue to it.
 |
| [Color Centers](color-centers) | Color centers are point defects in a crystal lattice that
selectively absorb visible light. When an ion is missing
(vacancy), extra (interstitial), or substituted by an impurity,
nearby electrons or holes become trapped. Their energy levels
often fall in the visible range, producing color. Color centers
form naturally via radiation, or via artificial irradiation
(which may fade with sunlight exposure — a key tell for
treated material).
 |
| [Charge Transfer](charge-transfer) | Charge transfer involves an electron hopping between adjacent
ions or between different energy states of the same ion, driven
by absorbed photons. Compared to d-d transitions, charge-
transfer absorption is typically 10²–10⁴ times stronger, so
even trace concentrations of the relevant elements can produce
saturated, vivid colors. Subtypes include metal–metal (MMCT),
oxygen–metal (OMCT), and metal–ligand (MLCT).
 |
