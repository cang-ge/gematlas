# Color Centers

## Overview

Color centers are point defects in a crystal lattice that
selectively absorb visible light. When an ion is missing
(vacancy), extra (interstitial), or substituted by an impurity,
nearby electrons or holes become trapped. Their energy levels
often fall in the visible range, producing color. Color centers
form naturally via radiation, or via artificial irradiation
(which may fade with sunlight exposure ‚ÄĒ a key tell for
treated material).


## Mechanism

Common color-center types:
- **F-centers** (anion vacancy trapping an electron): typical
  in fluorite and halite (rock salt)
- **V-centers** (cation vacancy trapping a hole): purple
  quartz (amethyst)
- **Impurity centers**: vacancy adjacent to a dopant ion,
  e.g. Fe‚Āī‚Āļ in amethyst
Color centers can form rapidly under laboratory irradiation,
but natural color centers accumulate over millions of years.
Detection of irradiated gems often relies on characteristic
short-wave UV luminescence signatures.
